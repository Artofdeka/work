Automating income verification to speed up application processing

Our team worked on a solution to enhance the credit lending onboarding journey with OCR, automated income verification, and bank statement retrieval functionalities. Leveraging automation to achieve seamless straight-through processing.

UX/UI design Absa
Part I

Embracing the challenge

Where it all started

The digital credit application journey presented a unique challenge for our business. Each customer must submit proof of income, disrupting seamless processing and necessitating manual verification by a sales consultant. This has adversely affected our conversion rates and customer retention.

Evaluating the As-Is journey

Our analysis of historical data across digital, voice, and physical channels revealed significant issues.

The main challenge in the current process is notably, income verification which occurs late in the application process, leading to high drop-off rates and longer turnaround times for applications approvals.

My role

As the UX/UI lead, I collaborated with Ayabulela (process engineer), Muano (senior engineer), and Lillian (business analyst), reporting to Sive (design project lead).

My responsibilities included research, wireframing, and iterative design to incorporate new insights and prototype solutions for stakeholder buy-in.

Support from UX designers, engineers, architects, analysts, and developers was crucial in refining and developing our designs collaboratively.

Business Challenges Addressed

Key challenges included 100% of digital channel applicants needing income proof, high drop-off rates due to verification delays, extended turnaround times affecting conversions, and inefficiencies from manual processing.

Customer-Centric Approach

Our approach aimed to balance individual customer needs with broader operational efficiencies, respecting their time and psychological experience during document submission.

Operational Enhancements

Introducing an orchestration layer streamlined the application process, automated product fulfillment, and minimized contact center hand-offs. The DEA functionality further reduced customer workload by retrieving bank statements, enhancing efficiency and reducing consultant dependency.

Part II

Process-driven design approach

Systems thinking with agility!

Before initiating design, we conducted comprehensive mapping of current processes to understand existing workflows thoroughly. This identified areas for improvement and laid the foundation for a process-led design approach, ensuring clear alignment between backend services and frontend impact.

Collaboration and Alignment

Partnering closely with Ayabulela, our process designer, and engineers from the design forum was pivotal throughout the project. This ongoing engagement facilitated efficient workflow and alignment across teams.

Analysis and Insights

Analyzing current process designs provided valuable operational insights, revealing gaps and opportunities for enhancement. Breaking down information into nodes and links made our internal mental models transparent and actionable.

Stakeholder Collaboration

Regular collaboration with stakeholders streamlined the design and implementation phases, enabling iterative improvements based on continuous feedback.

Our solution

Automation: We introduced automated income verification to replace manual back-office processing, enabling real-time credit decisions for digital channel applicants.

  • Streamlined operations with less dependency on manual verification.

Part V

Design, validate & iterate

Focusing on humans, not users

To create a product that deeply resonates with our customers on a meaningful level, we had to address their core human needs and experiences.

When customers apply for credit, their expectation is straightforward: approval and access to funds to meet various financial challenges. As designers, our role isn't to add stress but to rather respond with empathy and provide the support they need.

We used three prompts to guide customers on their next actions. From testing, we noticed customers misinterpreted 'email later,' thinking Absa would email them instead of them emailing. Changing 'Email later' to 'Skip' seem to do the trick.

A new issue arose: customers without documents or not banking with a DEA bank were unsure what to do. Adding a fourth instruction line helped, but since most customers still didn't read the prompts, it didn't effectively solve the problem.

We isolated the fourth instruction into its own distinct section with a dedicated title, ensuring it stood out prominently. While this adjustment reduced oversight, some customers still didn't read the initial three prompts, proceeding directly to the dropdown field to select their banks, and missing some of the key information that would later affect their experience.

We refined the design to a simplified yet effective layout. With a focus on real-time bank statement analysis as our main call to action, we had a prominently clear title, signaling quicker credit access. Customers who prefer not to have their statements fetched or prefer to submit them later had the option to use the 'Skip' button.

Giving customers control

The initial DEA call included four fields: bank name, account number, branch code, and account type. We aimed to streamline the process by minimizing customer input. Account type validation is not required, and universal branch codes eliminate the need for displaying them on the front-end.

Adding flexibility

We aimed to let customers add multiple accounts with fewer alternative actions, thus reducing tension and errors.

EXL Integration

Refining the validation panel

The decision to modify the income validation panel arose when we identified limitations in its performance on Salesforce screens. Although the current side panel functioned adequately, it fell short of meeting the evolving needs of our solution. While effective, it required enhancements to ensure the flexibility and usability demanded by our users.

Our initial step involved reconfiguring the panel to enable independent verification of each income by the sales consultant, a crucial functionality missing in the current setup for achieving success. Additionally, we subdivided it into collapsible subsections, granting consultants control over panel visibility to focus solely on relevant information. This enhanced flexibility not only empowered consultants but also reduced cognitive load.

The take away

Navigating Stakeholder Inputs and Decision Making

The project's complexity necessitated managing a variety of inputs from diverse stakeholders, requiring rigorous filtering and expectation management, especially when handling numerous ideas.

Managing stakeholder expectations was crucial, guiding our decision-making amidst occasional contention over which ideas to implement.

We prioritized reasoned choices over simply adhering to suggestions from senior executives, ensuring alignment with our principles.

Insights from user testing and usability assessments were invaluable in these deliberations.

Collaborative Approach for Best Practices

Close collaboration with the design and engineering core teams enabled adherence to best practices.

Working alongside colleagues in Technology, Architecture, and Subject Matter Experts (SMEs) from credit lending and scoring engine teams provided insights into the intricate relationship between front-end and back-end service calls.

Amid the project's complexity, we navigated multiple scenarios, prioritizing requirements based on their impact on the user journey.

Navigating Design Feedback: Learning from Early Challenges

Receiving early design feedback enabled us to implement precise measures from the outset. However, I've come to recognize the critical importance of seeking feedback even earlier, during the foundational stages.

At times, gathering input from a room filled with designers brimming with diverse ideas became overwhelming. The influx of ideas created a chaotic and impractical situation, pulling me deeper into complexity than anticipated. This compelled me to pause and reevaluate our approach.

Implementation and Results

The collaboration between the design team and process engineers ensured the new system was effectively integrated. Initial feedback indicates improved customer satisfaction and operational efficiency.

Summary

The project emphasized the necessity of a process-led design approach. The integration of process engineering from the start was crucial in achieving the project’s objectives.

A holistic approach to challenges

Throughout the project, my team and I encountered pushback when introducing new solutions that diverged from previous designs. We aimed to balance individual user needs with societal impact, respecting our customers' time and the psychological aspects of their experience.
